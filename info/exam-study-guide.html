1. 60-second elevator pitch

“This is an autonomous laser tracker built on a Raspberry Pi 4B. A USB webcam captures video, OpenCV finds a blue target by HSV colour thresholding, two PID controllers convert the pixel error into pan/tilt corrections, those go through an I²C-driven PCA9685 PWM controller to two DS3225 servos that aim the bracket, and a 3 V laser module on GPIO18 fires on operator confirmation. The whole thing runs from a tkinter control panel that launches each test or the full demo as a subprocess.”

2. The demo flow

  1. Open VNC into the Pi (or use the touchscreen). Double-click the desktop shortcut → control_panel.py launches.
  2. Click Initialize hardware — servos center, laser pin claimed LOW (OFF).
  3. Tick Enable laser controls (deliberate two-stage arming so the laser cannot fire by accident).
  4. Click the big green ▶ RUN FULL DEMO button. main.py spawns as a subprocess.
  5. In the OpenCV window:
    • A arms the laser → banner turns slate-blue (“ARMED — waiting for lock”).
    • Move the blue target in front of the camera. Bracket tracks it; once the pixel error is < 15 px on both axes the banner goes solid green (“LOCKED — press F to fire”).
    • F fires for 2.5 s (red banner), then 1 s cool-down.
    • Q exits cleanly: laser OFF, servos centered, camera released.

What is not on the board

  • No MOSFET — the original IRLZ44N driver was removed when the bare-diode plan was scrapped (problem 002). The 3 V module ships with its own driver IC, so a 3.3 V GPIO line is enough.
  • No MB102 power module — replaced by the LM2596 (problem 001).
  • No SPI / I²S / OneWire / ADC chips. The only digital bus used is I²C; the only digital GPIO is the laser pin. The camera is USB. Be ready to say this explicitly.

Wiring map

┌─→ DS3225 pan (PCA9685 ch 0) │ 12V 5A PSU ─→ LM2596 (5.0V) ─→ PCA9685┤ └─→ (LM2596 GND) ─→ GND └─→ DS3225 tilt (PCA9685 ch 1) Pi pin 2 (5V) ─→ PCA9685 VCC (logic power, ~50 mA) Pi pin 3 (SDA) ─→ PCA9685 SDA (I²C data, GPIO2) Pi pin 5 (SCL) ─→ PCA9685 SCL (I²C clock, GPIO3) Pi pin 6 (GND) ─→ PCA9685 GND (shared with LM2596 GND) Pi pin 12 (GPIO18) ─→ laser RED (+) (3.3 V digital drive) Pi pin 9 (GND) ─→ laser BLACK (−) (return) USB-A: LifeCam HD-3000 → any free Pi USB port (UVC, /dev/video0)

Two separate 5 V rails — why?

PCA9685 has two unrelated 5 V inputs:
  • VCC — chip logic. ~50 mA. Drawn from Pi pin 2 (clean, regulated, no servo noise).
  • V+ — servo header rail. Up to 2 A per servo at stall. Drawn from the LM2596 off the 12 V PSU.
These rails are not internally connected on the PCA9685. Separating them is what stops a servo current spike from browning out the Pi or corrupting I²C. Common GND across Pi, LM2596 OUT−, and PCA9685 GND is mandatory — without it I²C has no voltage reference and dies.

5. I²C deep dive — Pi ↔ PCA9685

Physical layer

  • Two wires + GND: SDA (data) and SCL (clock). Both are open-drain — devices can only pull the line LOW; resistors pull HIGH. The PCA9685 board has the pull-ups soldered on (typ. 10 kΩ to its own VCC).
  • Half-duplex, master-driven clock. Default clock on Raspberry Pi OS bus 1 is 100 kHz.
  • Pi is permanent master. PCA9685 is slave at 7-bit address 0x40 (default — set by 6 solder pads A0..A5 on the board, all open).

Frame structure

START | ADDR(7) R/W | ACK | REG(8) | ACK | DATA(8) | ACK | ... | STOP ↑ ↑ ↑ Pi writes PCA9685 PCA9685 0x40 + W=0 pulls SDA ACKs each byte LOW = ACK

Software stack

adafruit_servokit.ServoKit ← high-level "set angle" └─ adafruit_pca9685.PCA9685 ← register-level driver └─ adafruit_blinka ← CircuitPython compatibility shim └─ /dev/i2c-1 ← Linux i2c-dev character device └─ bcm2835-i2c ← Pi I²C peripheral driver in the kernel

What “kit.servo[0].angle = 135” actually triggers on the wire

  1. ServoKit maps 135° (with actuation_range=270, pulse_width_range=(500, 2500) µs) → 1500 µs pulse.
  2. At 50 Hz, one period is 20 ms = 4096 PCA9685 ticks. 1500 µs ≈ tick 307.
  3. The driver writes two 16-bit registers per channel: LEDn_ON_L / ON_H and LEDn_OFF_L / OFF_H. With ON=0, OFF=307, the channel goes HIGH at the start of each 20 ms cycle and LOW after 307 ticks.
  4. That single update is 5 byte transfers on the I²C bus (~0.4 ms at 100 kHz).
Quick sanity test from a terminal: 
i2cdetect -y 1
Output must show 40 at row 40 / col 0. Anything else = wiring problem (usually GND or SDA/SCL swap).

6. PWM & servo control (DS3225)

Why hobby servos need PWM

A hobby servo expects a pulse every 20 ms. The width of the HIGH portion (typically 500 – 2500 µs) is what tells the internal feedback loop the target shaft angle. The duty cycle as a percentage is not what matters — only the absolute pulse width.

┌──┐ ┌──┐ ┌──┐ ──┘ └───────────────────────────────────┘ └───────────────────────────────────┘ └── ↑──↑ ↑──↑ 500-2500 µs (next pulse exactly 20 ms later) HIGH = "this is your target"
DS3225 specValue
Pulse range500 µs – 2500 µs
Frequency50 Hz (period = 20 ms)
Mechanical range270°
Neutral / center pulse1500 µs → 135° electrical
Stall current~2 A at 6 V (per servo)
Voltage range4.8 – 6.8 V (we run 5.0 V)

Why actuation_range = 270 matters

adafruit_servokit.ServoKit defaults to actuation_range = 180. If you leave it at 180, then “135°” means halfway between 0 and 180, which the driver maps to halfway between min and max pulse — fine — but “200°” would be impossible. We explicitly set 270° so the calibrated mechanical limits (PAN_MAX = 220) are reachable.

Pi’s own PWM vs PCA9685 — why we don’t use GPIO PWM directly

  • The BCM2711 SoC has only two hardware PWM channels.
  • Software PWM (gpiozero / RPi.GPIO) is bit-banged from the CPU and suffers timing jitter under load — bad for servos.
  • PCA9685 generates 16 jitter-free PWM channels in its own clock; the Pi just sends the duty value over I²C.

7. GPIO direct drive — the laser

Pi BCM2711 GPIO18 ─── 3.3 V CMOS push-pull ───► Laser module RED (+) │ internal LDO + driver │ Pi GND ◄─────────────────────────────────────── Laser module BLACK (−)
  • Pin 12 on the header = BCM GPIO18.
  • HIGH = 3.3 V, sourcing ~10–15 mA into the module. LOW = 0 V, module dark.
  • No MOSFET because the module already has a current limiter inside. A MOSFET would only matter if you needed to switch a higher voltage or more current than a GPIO can provide.
  • Software: gpiozero.LED(18) → opens /dev/gpiochip0 through lgpio, claims pin 18 as output, drives it LOW initially. .on() → HIGH, .off() → LOW, .close() → releases the line.
Subprocess GPIO contention bug (be ready to explain): When the control panel launches main.py as a subprocess, the parent has to fully close gpiozero’s pin_factory before forking, otherwise the inherited chip file descriptor keeps GPIO18 “busy” in the child. The fix is in _on_run_demo: Device.pin_factory.close(); Device.pin_factory = None; time.sleep(0.3).

8. Camera path — USB · UVC · V4L2 · OpenCV

CMOS sensor ──► on-board ISP ──► USB UVC bulk endpoint ──► Pi USB controller │ kernel: uvcvideo driver │ userspace: V4L2 (/dev/video0) │ OpenCV: cv2.VideoCapture(0) │ numpy ndarray (BGR, 480×640×3)
  • UVC = USB Video Class. Standard descriptor set on the device side, so the kernel needs no vendor driver. The uvcvideo module handles every UVC webcam.
  • V4L2 = Video4Linux 2. The userspace API the kernel exposes — file ops + ioctls on /dev/video0. OpenCV uses it as one of its capture back-ends.
  • OpenCV may try GStreamer first (you’ll see “Cannot query video position” warning), then falls back to V4L2 — that’s normal.

Auto-exposure (AE) lock

In camera.init() we explicitly disable AE via V4L2 ioctls:

cap.set(cv2.CAP_PROP_AUTO_EXPOSURE, 1)     # 1 = manual (V4L2 convention)
cap.set(cv2.CAP_PROP_EXPOSURE, 250)        # exposure_absolute, V4L2 unit

Why: when the laser fires, its bright dot appears in the frame and the camera’s AE drops gain → the blue target dims → its HSV signature shifts → the detected centroid jitters ~10–30 px → bracket “dances”. Locking exposure stops the entire chain.

9. Detection — the HSV pipeline

  1. cv2.GaussianBlur(frame, (5,5), 0) — smooth sensor noise so single bright pixels don’t fragment the blob.
  2. cv2.cvtColor(blurred, cv2.COLOR_BGR2HSV) — switch to a colour space where hue (colour) is separated from value (brightness). RGB thresholding would break under any lighting change.
  3. cv2.inRange(hsv, HSV_LOWER, HSV_UPPER) — binary mask: 255 where the pixel is inside our blue range [79,76,0]–[105,255,255], 0 elsewhere.
  4. cv2.erode then cv2.dilate — morphological opening. Erode kills isolated noise pixels; dilate restores the real blob to its original size.
  5. cv2.findContours with RETR_EXTERNAL — outer contours only.
  6. Pick the largest contour; reject if its area is below MIN_CONTOUR_AREA = 200 px.
  7. cv2.moments(c) → centroid (m10/m00, m01/m00). Return (cx, cy).
Why HSV instead of RGB?

In RGB, “blue” covers a huge volume that also overlaps with darker shades of cyan, purple, navy, etc., and lighting changes shift the whole vector. In HSV the “colour” lives almost entirely in the hue channel, so a narrow hue band plus a sensible saturation floor reliably picks out a coloured object across a wide range of lighting and shadows.

Why not red?

OpenCV uses hue 0–179. Red sits at hue ≈ 0 and ≈ 179, so it wraps around — a single inRange can’t capture it. You’d need two masks OR’d together. Blue, green, orange, yellow all live in a single continuous hue band → one inRange works.

10. PID closed-loop control

One per axis

Pan and tilt are mechanically independent, so we run two independent PID controllers (the simple_pid library).

pan_error  = target_x - FRAME_CENTER_X      # +x = target right of center
tilt_error = target_y - FRAME_CENTER_Y      # +y = target BELOW center (OpenCV y is inverted)

pan_correction  = pan_pid(pan_error)        # degrees to add
tilt_correction = tilt_pid(tilt_error)

servo.move_pan (kit, current_pan  + pan_correction,  ramp=False)
servo.move_tilt(kit, current_tilt + tilt_correction, ramp=False)

Tuned gains (memorize)

ConstantValueWhy
Kp (both axes)0.017Sweet spot — fast enough to follow, calm on a stationary target.
Ki0No steady-state offset to correct; integral would wind up while the target is outside FOV.
Kd0Derivative amplifies detector centroid jitter into bracket motion.
Output limit±10°Caps per-frame swing → no current spike on the LM2596.
Deadband15 px≥ the detector centroid jitter range, so a stationary target doesn’t cause micro-corrections.

Coast and recenter (advanced)

  • Coast: if the target disappears mid-track, keep applying the last PID correction for up to 30 frames (~1 s) with a 0.95-decay each frame. Helps re-acquire a fast target that briefly leaves the FOV.
  • Recenter: if coast expires without re-acquisition, ease the bracket back to PAN/TILT center, 2° per frame. Without this the bracket would sit staring at a wall and never see the target again.

11. State machine — main.py

[Q] quit ↑ ┌─────────────────┐ [A] ┌──────────────────────┐ err < 15 px both axes │ DISARMED │───────►│ ARMED │────────────────────┐ │ gray, no fire │◄───────│ slate, awaiting lock │ │ └─────────────────┘ [A] └──────────────────────┘ │ ▲ ▼ │ COOLDOWN_S elapsed ┌──────────────────────┐ │ │ ARMED + LOCKED │ ┌──────────────────┐ │ green, [F] enabled │ │ COOLDOWN │ └──────────┬───────────┘ │ teal, 1.0 s │ │ [F] └──────────────────┘ ▼ ▲ ┌──────────────────────────┐ │ │ FIRING │ └──────────────│ red, laser HIGH, 2.5 s │ └──────────────────────────┘

Tracking runs in every state, including DISARMED, ARMED, FIRING, COOLDOWN. Only the laser is gated by state — the camera and PID never stop.

12. Async / threading — how things print without blocking

The teacher specifically flagged this: “anyone printing to a terminal must know the asynchronous side.” Be ready to answer in detail.

What we use (and why) — short version

MechanismUsed forWhere
logging module (not print)All runtime output; lets us add handlers (file, GUI text, etc.) without changing call sites.Every module
queue.QueueThread-safe hand-off of log messages from background loggers to the tk main thread.control_panel.py
Custom logging.HandlerIntercepts every log record and pushes its formatted string onto the queue.control_panel._QueueHandler
tk.root.after(200, _tick)Tkinter’s event-loop polling — runs _tick every 200 ms on the GUI thread. Drains the queue, refreshes labels, polls subprocesses.control_panel._tick
subprocess.PopenNon-blocking launch of cv2-based tools (tracking, tuner, calibrator, demo).control_panel._launch_script

Why this pattern matters

  • Tkinter is single-threaded. If you call log_text.insert() from a thread other than the one running mainloop(), the widget can crash or render garbled. So background work writes into a Queue (which has an internal lock) and the GUI thread drains it on its own schedule.
  • after() is cooperative async, not threads. It schedules _tick as the next event in the tk event loop — runs to completion, returns to the loop, comes back 200 ms later. No race conditions because there is only one thread touching the widgets.
  • Popen is the OS-level async. The Pi forks a child process, returns immediately, and we poll .returncode from _tick. Stdout/stderr of the child are redirected to ~/pi/last-subprocess.log so a crash leaves a readable trace.

Print vs logging

Why we never call print() from the modules
  • print writes straight to sys.stdout. If main.py is launched from the GUI as a subprocess, that stdout is captured into a file — invisible at runtime.
  • logging has levels (DEBUG / INFO / WARNING / ERROR), timestamps, the module name, and handlers. The GUI’s _QueueHandler picks up every record without the calling code knowing it’s being displayed.
  • print() is also buffered by default — pipe output to a file and lines may appear in bursts. We pass python3 -u to subprocesses to force unbuffered stdout for the log-tail feature to be useful.

Synchronous vs asynchronous I/O — the way to talk about it

  • Synchronous = the call blocks until the operation finishes. cv2.VideoCapture.read(), i2c_write(), time.sleep() — all synchronous.
  • Asynchronous = the call returns immediately; the work happens elsewhere and you find out later. subprocess.Popen, putting a message on a queue, scheduling a tk after() callback — all asynchronous from the caller’s point of view.
  • This project is mostly synchronous (main loop captures → detects → corrects → repeats). The async parts are only the GUI ↔ subprocess and logging ↔ GUI boundaries.

15. Safety mechanisms

  • Laser OFF by default. laser.init() drives the pin LOW before returning. The GUI hides laser controls behind an explicit “Enable laser controls” checkbox.
  • Two-stage arm. In main.py, the laser cannot fire until the operator presses A; even then, only when in_deadband is true (target locked).
  • try / finally everywhere. Every script ensures laser.cleanup() runs on exit, KeyboardInterrupt, or exception — so the laser never gets stuck ON.
  • Servo angle clamps. servo.move_pan / move_tilt apply the calibrated mechanical limits as the single point of safety. No caller can drive the bracket into a hard stop.
  • PID output clamp. ±10° per update — no single-frame slam into a limit, no current spike.
  • Power isolation. Servo current spikes cannot reach the Pi: their 5 V is from the LM2596, the Pi’s 5 V is from its own USB-C adapter. Common GND only.
  • Emergency stop. Big red button in the GUI: laser OFF, servos centered, devices released.

17. Electronics fundamentals — V / I / R / P

The teacher may probe whether you understand why the numbers in the schematic are what they are. Be ready to talk in volts, amps, watts, ohms.

Ohm's law and power

V = I · R (volts = amps · ohms) P = V · I = I²·R = V²/R (watts)

Why a buck converter (LM2596) and not a linear regulator?

A linear 12 V → 5 V regulator would dissipate (12 − 5) · I = 7 V · 1 A = 7 W as heat for a 1 A servo load. A buck converter switches the input on/off at high frequency and stores energy in an inductor — typically 85–92 % efficient, so the same 1 A load dissipates ~0.6 W instead of 7 W. No heat-sink needed.

Pull-up and pull-down resistors — the concept

  • A digital input is high-impedance when not driven; it floats and picks up noise. A resistor to a known rail (VCC or GND) defines the "default" state.
  • Pull-up: resistor from the line to VCC. Line defaults HIGH; an external switch to GND pulls it LOW.
  • Pull-down: resistor from the line to GND. Line defaults LOW; a switch to VCC pulls it HIGH.
  • I²C uses external pull-ups on the bus itself (not on inputs) because every device is open-drain — it can only pull LOW. The resistor restores HIGH when no device is active.
  • The original MOSFET-driven laser plan had a 100 kΩ pull-down on the gate so that during boot — when the GPIO is high-impedance for a few ms — the gate would stay LOW and the laser stay OFF. The current GPIO-direct design doesn't need this because the GPIO is driven LOW by laser.init() before anything else.

CMOS push-pull output (the Pi's GPIO)

A modern microcontroller GPIO has two transistors stacked between VCC and GND. To output HIGH, the top transistor conducts and pulls the pin to VCC. To output LOW, the bottom transistor conducts and pulls the pin to GND. This is "push-pull" — it can both source and sink current. The Pi GPIOs are CMOS push-pull at 3.3 V, rated for up to 16 mA per pin (and ≤ 50 mA total across all pins simultaneously).

18. Component internals (servo + CMOS + Pi SoC)

How a hobby servo (DS3225) works inside

PWM input pin │ ▼ ┌────────────────────────────────┐ │ Servo PCB: │ │ - measures incoming pulse │ │ width (500–2500 µs) │ │ - maps it to a target angle │ │ - compares against feedback │ │ pot voltage │ │ - drives H-bridge accordingly│ └─────────┬──────────────────────┘ │ ▼ H-bridge (4 transistors) │ ▼ brushed DC motor ──► gear train (reduction ~270:1) │ ▼ output shaft + potentiometer │ └──► feedback voltage to PCB

Closed-loop position control happens inside the servo, every ~20 ms. The Pi only commands an angle via the pulse width; the servo's own controller drives the motor and stops when the pot voltage matches the target. Stall happens when the H-bridge is at 100 % duty but the pot still doesn't reach target — typical when you command past a mechanical hard stop. Current spikes to ~2 A in that condition. Our software angle clamps in servo.py are what prevents stall by refusing to command an angle outside the calibrated safe range.

How a CMOS image sensor works inside

  1. Each pixel is a photodiode. Photons → electron-hole pairs → charge accumulates during the exposure window.
  2. At end of exposure, the charge is read out, amplified, and digitised by an on-chip ADC.
  3. A Bayer filter over the array means each pixel sees only one of R, G, B. The sensor's ISP de-mosaics by interpolating the missing two channels from neighbours — that's why a "640×480 colour image" comes from a Bayer array with the same total pixel count, not 3× more.
  4. The ISP also runs auto-exposure, auto-white-balance, gamma, sharpening, JPEG/YUV encoding, then ships the result over USB as a UVC stream.
  5. "Auto-exposure" is the ISP adjusting the exposure window (electronic shutter) frame-to-frame. Disabling AE via V4L2 means we tell the ISP to hold the exposure window at our specified value (250 in our case).

Why I²C for the PWM controller and not SPI?

  • Wire count. SPI needs 4 wires per slave (MOSI, MISO, SCK, CS); I²C needs 2 + GND total, regardless of how many slaves. We only have one slave, but I²C is still simpler to wire.
  • Speed. We don't need it. One servo update is ~5 bytes — at 100 kHz that's well under a millisecond. SPI's MHz speeds would be wasted.
  • The chip dictates it. The PCA9685 only has an I²C interface; if we wanted SPI we'd need a different chip (e.g. TLC5947 for LEDs, but no I²C).

What we'd need if we wanted to read an analog sensor

An external ADC chip, typically over I²C or SPI. Examples: ADS1115 (I²C, 4 channels, 16-bit), MCP3008 (SPI, 8 channels, 10-bit). Or a microcontroller (Arduino, RP2040) that has its own ADC and talks back to the Pi over UART or USB.

20. Image representation — numpy & OpenCV

How an OpenCV frame is stored

A frame from cap.read() is a numpy ndarray with shape (height, width, channels) = (480, 640, 3) and dtype uint8. That's 480 × 640 × 3 = 921 600 bytes per frame ≈ 900 KB. At 30 fps the camera produces ~27 MB/s of raw pixel data into RAM.

Why BGR and not RGB?

OpenCV was originally written for Intel cameras that delivered pixels in B-G-R byte order; the convention stuck. cv2.cvtColor(frame, cv2.COLOR_BGR2HSV) is the standard conversion. If you ever display an OpenCV frame in matplotlib, it'll look "wrong" (red and blue swapped) — that's the BGR/RGB mismatch.

HSV ranges in OpenCV (gotcha)

  • Hue: 0 – 179 (NOT 0 – 360 as in most graphics tools — OpenCV halves it so the channel fits in one byte).
  • Saturation: 0 – 255.
  • Value: 0 – 255.

So our HSV range [79, 76, 0] – [105, 255, 255] means "hue between 79 and 105 (roughly blue band), saturation at least 76 (rejects grey), value anywhere."

Centroid via image moments — the math

For a binary blob, the raw moments are:

m00 = Σ Σ I(x,y) (area of the blob) m10 = Σ Σ x · I(x,y) (sum of x coordinates, weighted) m01 = Σ Σ y · I(x,y) (sum of y coordinates, weighted) centroid = (m10 / m00 , m01 / m00)

OpenCV computes these in C; we just call cv2.moments(contour) and read three numbers. This is faster and more numerically stable than looping over pixels in Python.

The Python GIL — why it matters here

CPython has a Global Interpreter Lock: only one thread runs Python bytecode at a time. So using threading for CPU work (image processing) gives no speedup. It DOES help for I/O — a thread blocked in cap.read() releases the GIL while waiting, letting another thread run. We don't need that complication here because the loop is already fast enough to run synchronously at 30 fps.

"Async" in our project — what is and isn't

  • Synchronous: the main tracking loop, every I²C transaction, every cap.read(), every laser_dev.on().
  • Asynchronous in the application sense:
    • Log records produced by background code → queued → drained by the GUI later.
    • Subprocess started, returns immediately, polled later.
    • Tk after() schedules _tick for "later."
  • Not used: Python's asyncio (async def / await), concurrent.futures.

22. gpiozero internals & the pin_factory

gpiozero is the high-level Python library; under it there's a "pin factory" that talks to the actual hardware.

gpiozero.LED(18) │ ▼ Device.pin_factory (singleton, lazily created) │ ▼ LGPIOPin ←── default on Raspberry Pi OS Bookworm │ ▼ lgpio C library ←── opens /dev/gpiochip0 │ ▼ Linux kernel gpiolib │ ▼ BCM2711 GPIO controller registers (memory-mapped) │ ▼ Physical pin 18

Why the pin_factory matters for subprocesses

When control_panel.py launches main.py as a subprocess, the child inherits open file descriptors via fork(). Even after we call laser.cleanup() in the parent (which closes the LED object), the pin_factory still holds an open handle to /dev/gpiochip0. That handle is inherited; the child sees GPIO18 as "busy" and crashes with lgpio.error: 'GPIO busy'.

The fix in _on_run_demo / _on_boresight:

from gpiozero import Device
if Device.pin_factory is not None:
    Device.pin_factory.close()    # release chip handle
    Device.pin_factory = None     # forces re-creation on next LED()
time.sleep(0.3)                   # let kernel state settle
subprocess.Popen([...])           # NOW it's safe to fork

Q. Why is there a common ground between the Pi, LM2596 and PCA9685?
A. I²C is a single-ended bus. SDA and SCL voltages are measured against ground; if the two ends of the bus don’t share a 0 V reference, “HIGH” on the Pi might look like “LOW” at the PCA9685 — or vice versa. Without common GND the bus simply doesn’t work.
Q. Why no MOSFET on the laser?
A. The 3 V laser module is self-contained — it has its own driver IC and current limiter on board. It needs ≈ 10–15 mA at 3 V, which is comfortably inside the Pi GPIO’s 16 mA-per-pin spec. A MOSFET is for switching loads the GPIO can’t handle directly (high current, higher voltage, inductive loads). None of those apply here, so a MOSFET would be pure complexity with no benefit.
Q. What would change if we used a bare laser diode?
A. A bare 5 mW diode draws ~30 mA continuously and needs an external current-limiting resistor plus a higher rail than 3.3 V. Then the GPIO can’t drive it directly — current is borderline and the voltage is wrong — so we’d add a low-side N-channel MOSFET (e.g. IRLZ44N) with a gate-pulldown to ground so a floating GPIO during boot doesn’t turn the laser on. We considered that path originally; it was abandoned when we got the integrated module.
Q. Why 50 Hz on the PCA9685?
A. Because that’s what hobby servos expect — one pulse every 20 ms. The pulse width (500–2500 µs) carries the position command. The PCA9685’s frequency is set globally for all 16 channels; we leave it at 50 Hz because both DS3225s are on it. If we ever mixed servos and LED drivers on the same chip, we’d need separate chips.

I²C

Q. Describe the I²C transaction that happens when you set a servo angle.
A. The Pi acts as master. It issues a START condition, sends the 7-bit slave address 0x40 with the write bit, the PCA9685 ACKs by pulling SDA low on the 9th clock. The Pi then sends the register address (e.g. LED0_ON_L), ACK, then the data byte (low 8 bits of the ON tick), ACK; followed by the ON_H byte, OFF_L byte, OFF_H byte, each with an ACK. Then a STOP. With a 12-bit duty value at 50 Hz, that’s how one channel update gets across — about 5 bytes on the wire, ~0.4 ms at 100 kHz.
Q. Are SDA and SCL push-pull?
A. No — they’re open-drain. Devices can only pull the line LOW; pull-up resistors restore HIGH. That’s how multiple devices can share the bus without short-circuiting each other and how the slave can stretch the clock by holding SCL low. On our PCA9685 board the pull-ups are already soldered on (typ. 10 kΩ to its VCC).
Q. What if you connected two PCA9685s?
A. The address pads A0..A5 let you set the 7-bit address from 0x40 to 0x7F. Solder a couple of bridges on the second board to give it, say, 0x41. Bus arbitration is implicit because the Pi is the sole master — each transaction targets one address.
Q. How would you verify I²C is alive without running the program?
A. i2cdetect -y 1 on the Pi. The expected output is a single 40 at row 40 / column 0 — meaning a slave responds to address 0x40 on bus 1.

PWM / servos

Q. Why isn’t the Pi generating PWM itself?
A. The BCM2711 has only two hardware PWM channels. Software-PWM is bit-banged from the CPU and jitters under any load — that’s visible as servo twitching. The PCA9685 has its own clock that produces 16 jitter-free channels; the Pi only sends register updates over I²C.
Q. What is the relationship between the 500–2500 µs pulse and the 270° range?
A. 500 µs → angle 0°, 2500 µs → angle 270°, linearly between. So the centre 1500 µs → 135°. Our calibrated mechanical limits are 50° and 220° on pan, 115° and 205° on tilt — those are the safe travel ranges of the assembled bracket, not the servo’s electrical limits.
Q. Why ramp=False in the tracking loop?
A. The ramp helper steps in 2° / 50 ms increments. A 20° correction would block the loop for half a second — the camera would back up, frames pile up, the loop feels laggy. With ramp=False we send a single PWM command and the DS3225’s own mechanical slew rate (~1°/12 ms) smooths the motion. The PID’s ±10° output limit prevents abrupt jumps.

Code / control flow

Q. Walk me through one frame.
A. (Path A above, summarised.) capture_frame grabs a BGR ndarray from the webcam. detector.detect blurs, converts to HSV, masks with inRange, morphological opening, picks the largest contour, computes its centroid. The centroid is fed to tracker.update, which subtracts frame centre to get a pixel error, runs each axis through its PID controller, clamps the per-frame correction, and calls servo.move_pan and move_tilt. Those funnel through ServoKit → PCA9685 → PWM → DS3225. The bracket moves, the loop returns and grabs the next frame.
Q. What happens if the detector loses the target?
A. tracker.update receives None from detector.detect. First it tries to coast: keep applying the last PID correction for up to 30 frames with a 0.95 decay. If coast expires without re-acquiring, it switches to recenter mode and steps the bracket back to PAN/TILT centre at 2°/frame. The moment the target reappears, normal PID resumes.
Q. Why are there two PIDs and not one?
A. Pan and tilt are mechanically independent axes — the pan servo cannot affect tilt error and vice versa. Treating them as one MIMO problem would be over-engineering. Two separate SISO PIDs converge faster and are easier to tune.
Q. Why no integral term?
A. We have no constant bias to correct: gravity doesn’t pull the bracket, and the target isn’t moving at a constant rate that a P-controller would lag behind. An integral term would only wind up while the target is outside the deadband (or outside the FOV) and then overshoot when it re-acquires. P-only is the right choice here.
Q. Why does tracker.update not update PID state when there’s no target?
A. Because feeding the PID a phantom “error = 0” every frame the target is missing would corrupt the integral and derivative terms. With no target, we skip the PID call entirely and either coast or recenter.

Async / printing

Q. How does the GUI log pane stay responsive while modules print messages?
A. Two pieces. Every module uses Python’s logging. The control panel installs a custom handler — _QueueHandler — on the root logger. Every log record gets formatted into a string and pushed onto a thread-safe queue.Queue. The GUI runs root.after(200, _tick), which schedules _tick on the tkinter event loop. _tick drains the queue into the scrolling text widget, refreshes status labels, polls subprocesses, then re-schedules itself. So background log producers never touch tk widgets directly — they always go through the queue.
Q. Is that multi-threading?
A. Partly. The GUI thread is the only one touching tk widgets — that’s safe by design. The producers (logger callsites inside the main code, or inside subprocesses’ stdout pipe) can be in different threads or processes; the queue.Queue object is the synchronisation primitive (it’s internally locked). after() itself is not threads — it’s cooperative scheduling on the tk event loop, an OS-level select() pattern under the hood.
Q. What’s the difference between synchronous and asynchronous I/O?
A. Synchronous = the caller blocks until the operation finishes. cap.read(), i2c_write, time.sleep all block. Asynchronous = the caller returns immediately and the work is done elsewhere; you find out later via a callback, a queue, polling, or events. In this project the main tracking loop is synchronous (deliberately, to keep frame timing tight), and the GUI ↔ subprocess + logging ↔ GUI boundaries are asynchronous.
Q. Why don’t you use print()?
A. Three reasons. (1) print writes straight to stdout — if main.py is launched as a GUI subprocess, that stdout is redirected to a log file and the operator can’t see it live. (2) logging has levels (INFO / WARNING / ERROR), timestamps, and the module name baked in. (3) Handlers are pluggable: the GUI’s _QueueHandler captures every record across every module without changing a single call site. print is also line-buffered by default in pipes, which would delay subprocess crash messages — we pass python3 -u to subprocesses to force unbuffered stdout for the log-tail feature.
Q. Why isn’t the project using asyncio?
A. asyncio shines when you have many concurrent I/O-bound tasks — web sockets, network requests. Our hot loop is CPU-bound (OpenCV ops on a numpy array) and has exactly one I/O operation per frame (camera + servo write). Adding asyncio would just add overhead. The tkinter after() + queue.Queue + Popen trio handles every async need the project actually has.

Camera / detection

Q. Why HSV and not RGB?
A. HSV separates colour (hue) from brightness (value), so we can write a hue range that picks out “blue” independently of lighting. In RGB, lighting shifts the whole vector — what’s “blue” at noon is a different RGB point under fluorescent light, so the threshold breaks.
Q. Why not red?
A. OpenCV’s hue scale is 0–179. Red sits at both ends — around 0 and around 179 — so a single inRange can’t capture it. You’d have to OR two masks. Blue (hue ≈ 100) sits in a single continuous band, so one threshold works.
Q. Why is auto-exposure disabled?
A. When the laser fires, its bright dot appears in the frame. The camera’s AE algorithm sees a brighter scene and drops gain to compensate. That dims the blue target → its HSV signature shifts → the centroid jitters by 10–30 px → the bracket micro-corrects and visibly dances. Locking exposure at a fixed value via V4L2 ioctl removes the AE response entirely; the image stays stable during fire and the detector centroid is rock-solid.
Q. What’s “morphological opening” for?
A. Erode followed by dilate. Erosion strips one ring of pixels off every white blob — kills isolated noise specks completely. Dilation adds one ring back — restores the size of the real (large) blob. Net result: small noise gone, real target unchanged.

Misc / curveballs

Q. What is the boresight offset?
A. The pixel offset between where the camera is pointing (frame centre) and where the laser dot actually lands. If the laser and camera are mounted parallel but with a vertical/horizontal gap, the laser dot at finite distance won’t coincide with frame centre. The fix is to bias the PID setpoint by (BORESIGHT_X_OFFSET, BORESIGHT_Y_OFFSET). In our build the laser is physically taped on top of the camera with its emission axis aligned to the optical axis, so the offset is essentially zero — but calibrate_boresight.py exists to measure and write it if alignment ever drifts.
Q. What is the “coast” mode?
A. If the target disappears mid-track — typically because the user moved it too fast for the bracket — instead of freezing immediately we keep applying the last PID correction for up to 30 frames (~1 s) with a 0.95 decay each frame. This carries the bracket forward in the same direction; if the target re-enters the FOV, normal PID resumes. Without this, fast targets would be lost permanently the moment they crossed an edge.
Q. Could you describe the laser test path step by step?
A. (Path B above.) Keystroke F lands inside cv2.waitKey, the state machine checks ARMED + LOCKED + not COOLDOWN, calls laser.fire(laser_dev) which calls laser_dev.on() from gpiozero. gpiozero’s LED class delegates to its pin_factory — on Bookworm that’s lgpio, which writes to /dev/gpiochip0, which the kernel turns into a register write on the BCM2711’s GPIO peripheral. Pin 18 goes HIGH at 3.3 V; the 3 V module’s internal driver enables the diode. 2.5 s later main.py calls laser.off and the pin goes LOW.
